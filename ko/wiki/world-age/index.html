세계의 시대

세계의 시대는 Wheel of Heaven 코퍼스에서 봄 분점이 황도 십이궁 중 하나를 통과하는 기간 — 약 2,160년 — 을 가리킨다. 열두 개의 세계의 시대가 하나의 대년을 이루며, 이는 본 프로젝트 우주 연대론의 기본 단위이다.

A World Age — also called Precessional Age, Astrological Age, Zodiacal Age, or Great Month — is one of twelve intervals of approximately 2,160 years composing the Great Year, the ~25,920-year cycle produced by the precession of Earth's axis through the twelve constellations of the zodiac. The framework operates the World Age as the principal organizing temporal-cosmological unit of the corpus narrative: each age is read as an engineering-operational phase of the alliance's twenty-two-thousand-year project on Earth, with specific tasks, characteristic interventions, and a doubled astronomical signature encoded in the religious-iconographic tradition of the cultures the alliance cultivated during that age.

The mechanism is straightforward astronomy. Earth's rotational axis is tilted at approximately 23.5° relative to the plane of its orbit around the sun. The axis is not fixed in space; it traces out a slow conical wobble, like the wobble of a spinning top, completing one full cone every ~25,920 years. The wobble is produced principally by the gravitational torque exerted on Earth's equatorial bulge by the sun and moon. The rate of the precessional motion is approximately 50 arcseconds per year — which is the angular width of the full moon traversed over roughly thirty-six years, small enough to escape casual observation but large enough that, across millennia, it produces measurable changes in the apparent position of the stars. The most consequential effect of this wobble for the precessional-age framework is the gradual westward motion of the vernal equinox — the point in the sky where the sun rises on the first day of spring — against the background of the fixed stars. The vernal-equinox sun, observed at the moment of spring's beginning, rises against a different region of the zodiacal background in different epochs. The constellation against which it rises serves as the signature of the epoch. Each constellation occupies approximately one-twelfth of the zodiacal circle, so the vernal equinox spends approximately 2,160 years in each constellation before crossing into the next. This 2,160-year interval is the World Age.

The framework reads the precessional cycle as the alliance's principal temporal-cosmological framework — the calendrical backbone within which the entire creation project on Earth has unfolded. The Capricorn age opened the project at approximately 21,810 BCE, when an alliance expedition arrived at Earth and began the planetary-engineering work required to prepare the world for the eventual creation of life. Eleven subsequent ages — Sagittarius, Scorpio, Libra, Virgo, Leo, Cancer, Gemini, Taurus, Aries, Pisces, and the present Aquarius — have each contributed a specific phase to the unfolding work. The corpus's twelve main chapters walk through these ages in sequence. Each age is treated not as a period of mystical-astrological symbolism but as an operational phase of an extended engineering project, with tasks appropriate to its position in the longer arc and with religious-iconographic signatures left in the cultures of the period that preserve the astronomical identity of the age across the millennia during which the original meaning would otherwise be lost.

The framework's distinctive contribution within the broader landscape of precessional thought is fourfold. First, the precessional ages are read as engineering units that the alliance operationally employs — the zodiac as calendrical tool of the project rather than as mystical-symbolic system superimposed on it. Second, the doubled signature principle (each age encoding its astronomical identity in both its current sign and its zodiacal opposite) is given systematic treatment as the mechanism by which the astronomical content survives transmission through religious traditions whose carriers no longer understand what they are transmitting. Third, the twelve precessional ages are aligned with twelve framework-articulated phases of the broader project (the pre-human ages of planetary preparation; the human-creation age of Leo; the post-Eden ages of civilizational development; the Piscean age of universal distribution; the present Aquarian age of inheritance), producing a tightly integrated chronological-narrative structure. Fourth, the corpus draws explicitly on the precessional reconstruction articulated by Giorgio de Santillana and Hertha von Dechend in Hamlet's Mill (1969), extending their cross-cultural framework through the additional interpretive moves the corpus introduces.

The current World Age is the Age of Aquarius. The corpus chronology dates the Aquarian opening at 1950 CE, with the source-anchored alternative dating of 1946 CE marking the age's operational opening (the year of Vorilhon's birth and of various other inaugural events). The age runs to approximately 4110 CE — at which point the cycle returns to a new Capricorn, opening the second Great Year of the framework's larger temporal architecture. The corpus reads this transition as the closure of the first Great Year (the cycle during which the alliance's creation project on Earth has unfolded) and the opening of the second Great Year (the cycle during which humanity itself becomes the creating civilization, extending the work to other worlds across the galactic neighborhood). The detailed treatment of the broader Cosmic Chain framework lives in the Cosmic Chain entry; the World Age entry's contribution is registering the precessional-temporal architecture within which that chain operates.

The reading is grounded across two distinct source families. The precessional-mechanical content draws on mainstream astronomy, accepted across the scholarly tradition since Hipparchus's discovery of precession in approximately 130 BCE and given its modern gravitational explanation by Newton in the Principia (1687). The precessional-cultural content draws on Hamlet's Mill and the broader archaeoastronomical tradition, with substantial framework-specific extension. The framework's epistemic status is substantial-source-grounding-with-substantial-corpus-extension — the mechanism is mainstream science; the cross-cultural reading is Hamlet's Mill and successors; the alliance-engineering reading is the corpus's distinctive contribution.

Conventional understanding

The precessional-age framework operates within multiple distinct disciplinary contexts, each engaging the concept differently.

The astronomical mechanism

Mainstream astronomy treats the precession of Earth's axis as a well-understood physical phenomenon. The basic mechanics:

The axial wobble. Earth's rotational axis is tilted at approximately 23.44° relative to the ecliptic plane (the plane of Earth's orbit around the sun). The axis is not stationary in space; it traces out a slow conical motion, completing one full cone every approximately 25,772 years (the most precise contemporary measurement; the rounded figure of 25,920 years used in the corpus framework is the traditional approximation that yields the convenient 2,160-year-per-age unit).

The gravitational cause. The wobble is produced principally by the gravitational torque exerted on Earth's equatorial bulge by the sun and moon. Earth is not a perfect sphere; it bulges at the equator due to its rotational flattening. The sun and moon, pulling unequally on the near and far sides of this bulge, exert a torque that attempts to align Earth's equatorial plane with the ecliptic plane. Earth's rotational angular momentum resists this alignment, producing precessional motion of the rotational axis (analogous to the precession of a spinning top under gravitational torque). Newton provided the first mathematical treatment in the Principia (1687).

The rate. The precessional rate is approximately 50.29 arcseconds per year (the contemporary measurement). At this rate, the celestial pole traces its full circuit in approximately 25,772 years.

The effect on the equinoxes. The vernal and autumnal equinoxes — the points where the sun's apparent path (the ecliptic) crosses the celestial equator — move slowly westward against the background of the fixed stars at the same rate as the axial precession. This is why the phenomenon is called the "precession of the equinoxes."

The effect on the pole stars. The celestial north pole — the point in the sky around which the stars appear to rotate — moves slowly across the sky as the axis traces its conical motion. The current north pole star is Polaris (alpha Ursae Minoris), but Polaris was not always the pole star: in approximately 3000 BCE, the pole star was Thuban (alpha Draconis); in approximately 12,000 CE, the pole star will be Vega (alpha Lyrae). The cycle continues.

The effect on the zodiacal calendar. Because the vernal equinox moves westward through the zodiac at approximately 2,160 years per constellation, the constellation against which the equinox sun rises changes across millennia. Approximately 2,000 years ago, the equinox sun rose against the constellation Pisces. It is now transitioning toward Aquarius. The exact boundary depends on how the constellational regions are defined (the constellations are not equal in size, and modern astronomers use precise constellational boundaries defined by the International Astronomical Union in 1930; older astrological traditions use idealized 30°-per-sign divisions).

The Hipparchus discovery

The precession of the equinoxes was first explicitly identified in the Western tradition by Hipparchus of Nicaea (approximately 190-120 BCE) in the second century BCE. Hipparchus, working in Rhodes, compared his own measurements of star positions with measurements made by earlier Greek astronomers (notably Timocharis and Aristyllus, working in Alexandria in the third century BCE) and identified a small but consistent shift of approximately 1° per century — what we now know as the precessional motion. Hipparchus's work is preserved principally through Ptolemy's Almagest (second century CE), since Hipparchus's own writings have been substantially lost.

Mainstream history of astronomy generally treats Hipparchus as the original discoverer of precession. The framework offers a different reading (treated under Modern reinterpretations below): the precessional cycle was tracked implicitly through religious-iconographic symbolism for millennia before Hipparchus's explicit identification, with Hipparchus marking the first explicit articulation rather than the first discovery.

The Newtonian gravitational explanation

The mathematical-physical explanation of precession was provided by Isaac Newton in Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica (1687). Newton showed that the gravitational torque on Earth's equatorial bulge from the sun and moon produces the observed precessional motion at the observed rate, providing the theoretical framework that connects the kinematic phenomenon (the axial wobble) to the dynamical cause (gravity acting on the non-spherical Earth). The Newtonian framework remains the basis for modern precessional theory, refined by subsequent developments in celestial mechanics.

Mainstream astrology and the precessional-age framework

Mainstream Western astrology operates principally through the tropical zodiac, where signs are defined by seasonal positions (Aries begins at the vernal equinox, regardless of which constellation is actually behind the equinox sun) rather than by actual constellational positions. The tropical framework decouples astrological signs from the precessional motion — Aries is always Aries in the tropical zodiac, even though the equinox sun no longer actually rises against the constellation Aries.

The precessional-age framework operates principally through the sidereal zodiac, where signs are tied to actual constellational positions. In the sidereal framework, the question "what age are we in?" is answered by checking which constellation the equinox sun actually rises against. The current sidereal answer: the equinox sun is transitioning from rising against Pisces to rising against Aquarius, which is why the present is described as the transition from the Age of Pisces to the Age of Aquarius.

Both frameworks are internally consistent. The tropical framework is principally used for natal-chart astrology (where the seasonal-position framework has practical advantages for the kind of work natal astrology does). The sidereal framework is principally used in Vedic astrology (the Hindu astrological tradition, which preserves the older sidereal framework) and in precessional-age engagement (where the actual constellational position matters). The framework operates principally through the sidereal-precessional engagement.

The cross-cultural age-counting traditions

The cross-cultural distribution of cyclical-cosmological frameworks is itself a substantial scholarly observation, addressed in the Comparative observations section below. The principal traditions: Hindu yugas; Mesoamerican Long Count; Mesopotamian sar; Egyptian Sothic cycles; Greek Great Year (Plato, Hesiod's ages); Persian millennial system; Chinese cosmic cycles; Norse cyclical cosmology; various others. The corpus framework reads this cross-cultural distribution as evidence that the precessional cycle has been tracked across virtually every major civilization, in forms specific to each tradition but converging on the cyclical-cosmological character.

Hamlet's Mill as foundational scholarly work

Giorgio de Santillana (1902-1974), a historian of science at MIT, and Hertha von Dechend (1915-2001), a historian of science at the University of Frankfurt, published Hamlet's Mill: An Essay on Myth and the Frame of Time (Gambit, Boston, 1969). The work is the principal scholarly anchor for the corpus's precessional framework. Its principal argument: the precessional cycle was tracked by virtually every major ancient civilization through religious-iconographic and mythological symbolism, with the resulting body of cross-cultural mythological material preserving substantial astronomical content despite its surface appearance as religious-mythological narrative. The work assembles cross-cultural evidence from Mesopotamian, Egyptian, Greek, Indian, Chinese, Mesoamerican, Polynesian, and various other traditions, demonstrating consistent encoding of precessional knowledge across geographically and temporally distant civilizations.

The framework operates substantively within the Hamlet's Mill tradition while extending it through several additional interpretive moves: the doubled-signature principle (treated in detail in the Doubled Signature entry), the alliance-engineering reading of the precessional ages, and the integration with Raëlian source material. The detailed treatment of Hamlet's Mill lives in the Hamlet's Mill entry when written; the World Age entry's contribution is registering the work as foundational scholarly anchor.

The precessional direction

The vernal equinox precesses backward through the zodiac — that is, the equinox sun rises against constellations in the reverse of the order in which the sun moves through them across the year. The conventional astrological order (Aries → Taurus → Gemini → ...) reflects the sun's annual motion through the zodiac; the precessional order (Capricorn → Sagittarius → Scorpio → ...) reflects the slow westward motion of the equinox point against the zodiacal background. This inversion is one of the principal sources of confusion for popular engagement with precessional-age content.

The framework uses 2,160 years as the standard unit, recognizing that boundary-dating decisions involve some interpretive flexibility.

The doubled signature principle

Each precessional age encodes its astronomical signature in doubled form — referencing both the current sign (the constellation against which the equinox sun rises) and the opposite sign (the constellation 180° away on the zodiacal circle). The doubled signature ensures the astronomical content survives transmission across centuries during which the original meaning may be lost. The pattern operates across the historical record: Taurus paired with Scorpio; Aries paired with Libra; Pisces paired with Virgo; the present Aquarius paired with Leo.

The Hindu yuga system

The Hindu cosmological tradition preserves the most elaborate cyclical-temporal architecture of any major religious tradition.

The four-yuga cycle. The principal Hindu cyclical unit is the mahayuga (great age), composed of four yugas:

  • Krita Yuga (also Satya Yuga, "Age of Truth"): 1,728,000 years in the standard reckoning; the golden age of perfect dharma
  • Treta Yuga ("Third Age"): 1,296,000 years; the age of three-quarter dharma
  • Dvapara Yuga ("Second Age"): 864,000 years; the age of half dharma
  • Kali Yuga ("Age of Kali," the strife-age): 432,000 years; the age of one-quarter dharma — the present age

The full mahayuga totals 4,320,000 years (the four yuga numbers in 4:3:2:1 ratio). The current kali yuga is conventionally reckoned as having begun at 3102 BCE (the traditional date of the Mahabharata war).

The larger cosmic cycles. The mahayuga nests within larger cycles:

  • Manvantara ("age of a Manu"): 71 mahayugas, or 306,720,000 years
  • Kalpa ("day of Brahma"): 14 manvantaras plus various transitional periods, totaling approximately 4.32 billion years
  • Mahakalpa ("life of Brahma"): 100 years of Brahma, with each Brahma year containing 360 such days and nights

The precessional-encoding reading. As discussed under Modern reinterpretations, Sri Yukteswar's framework (extended by various subsequent scholars) proposes that the original Hindu yuga system used substantially shorter cycles aligned with the precessional cycle, with the larger numbers introduced through later interpretive elaboration. The reading is contested but registers substantial structural alignment with the broader precessional framework.

The framework reading. The corpus reads the Hindu yuga system as preserving substantial cultural memory of the cyclical-cosmological framework within distinctive Hindu cosmological framing. The substantial elaboration of the larger cycles registers the Hindu tradition's distinctive scaling of the broader cyclical pattern.

The Mesoamerican Long Count calendar

The Mesoamerican (principally Mayan, with substantial broader Mesoamerican engagement) Long Count calendar preserves substantive cyclical-temporal architecture.

The Long Count units:

  • Kin: 1 day
  • Winal: 20 kin (20 days)
  • Tun: 18 winal (360 days)
  • Katun: 20 tun (7,200 days, ~19.7 years)
  • Baktun: 20 katun (144,000 days, ~394 years)

The Long Count cycle. The full Long Count cycle of 13 baktuns equals 1,872,000 days, or approximately 5,125 years. The current cycle is conventionally reckoned as having begun at 3114 BCE (the Mayan creation date) and as having completed at December 21, 2012 CE (the much-discussed "Mayan apocalypse" date that produced substantial popular engagement). The completion of the 13-baktun cycle in 2012 marked the transition to a new Long Count cycle.

The structural relationship to precession. The 5,125-year Long Count cycle approximates one-fifth of the 25,920-year precessional cycle. The structural relationship has produced substantive scholarly engagement (principally John Major Jenkins's work) proposing that the Long Count encodes precessional content. The reading is contested but registers structural parallels worth noting.

The framework reading. The corpus reads the Mesoamerican Long Count as preserving substantive cyclical-cosmological content within distinctive Mesoamerican framing, with substantive structural parallels to the broader precessional framework.

The Egyptian Sothic cycle

The Egyptian astronomical tradition preserves the Sothic cycle — the period required for the heliacal rising of Sirius (the brightest star in the night sky) to return to the same position in the Egyptian civil calendar.

The Sothic cycle. The Egyptian civil calendar of 365 days drifted relative to the actual astronomical year (approximately 365.25 days), producing a one-day shift every four years. The accumulated shift returned to alignment after 1,461 Egyptian civil years, or 1,460 actual years — the Sothic cycle.

The structural relationship to precession. The Sothic cycle is not directly a precessional unit (it's principally a calendrical-drift unit), but the broader Egyptian astronomical tradition's substantial multi-millennial observational record has produced substantive scholarly engagement with precessional content. The Egyptian temple alignments across multiple dynasties show patterns consistent with precessional-aware adjustment.

The framework reading. The corpus reads the Egyptian astronomical-calendrical tradition as preserving substantive observational engagement with celestial cycles, with the broader Egyptian framework operating within the broader pre-Hipparchean precessional-awareness pattern.

Produced by the precession of Earth's axis — the slow conical wobble of the rotational axis caused principally by gravitational torque on Earth's equatorial bulge from the sun and moon, completing one full cycle every ~25,920 years at a rate of ~50 arcseconds per year
zodiacal_basis
The twelve constellations of the zodiac, against which the vernal equinox sun rises in succession across the precessional cycle; the equinox moves backward (westward) through the zodiac at one sign per ~2,160 years
precessional_direction
Backward through the zodiac (Capricorn → Sagittarius → Scorpio → Libra → Virgo → Leo → Cancer → Gemini → Taurus → Aries → Pisces → Aquarius → back to Capricorn)
great_year_total
12 ages × 2,160 years = 25,920 years (the complete precessional cycle, also called the Platonic Year, Great Year, or annus magnus)
current_age
Age of Aquarius (1950 CE corpus boundary; 1946 CE source-anchored opening) running to ~4110 CE
corpus_operational_period
From the Capricorn-age opening at -21,810 to the Aquarian-age present (approximately 22,000 years), spanning twelve full precessional ages and constituting essentially one complete Great Year of the alliance's creation project
foundational_scholarship
Giorgio de Santillana and Hertha von Dechend, Hamlet's Mill: An Essay on Myth and the Frame of Time (Gambit, 1969) — the principal scholarly anchor for the corpus's precessional framework
distinguished_from
Mainstream astrology's tropical-zodiac framework (where signs are tied to seasonal positions rather than to actual constellational positions, decoupled from the precessional motion); the New Age movement's broader Aquarian-age engagement (which operates with various boundary-dating conventions and various interpretive emphases); the broader 'astrological age' popular designation (which often conflates precessional and tropical frameworks)
status_in_framework

Wheel of Heaven은 고대 세계의 창조 전승 — 라엘리안, 성경, 메소포타미아, 그 외 — 을 하나의 작업 가설을 통해 읽어내는 열린 다국어 읽기입니다. 그 가설이란, 엘로힘이라 불리는 존재가 다른 곳에서 온 작은 선진 인류 문명이었으며, 그들이 이곳에 와서 지구상의 생명을 설계하고 종교 문헌에 그 기록을 남겼다는 것입니다. 이 읽기는 세차 주기의 열두 시대에 걸쳐 펼쳐집니다. 이만 이천 년 전 프로젝트의 시작에서, 현재의 드러남의 시대를 지나, 다음 대년(大年)이 여는 긴 미래까지 이어집니다. 이 사이트는 오픈소스이며 출처에 충실하고, 작업이 검증될 수 있도록 만들어졌습니다.
